Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Zonglin Zhang (zoz002@ucsd.edu), La Li (ax010602@ucsd.edu), Vincent Gao (v2gao@ucsd.edu)

Proposition: The CCRB complaint process appeared to protect higher-ranking NYPD officers from substantiated findings.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score: -1.0 - I changed the raw substantiation rates into gaps from the Police Officer rate. That makes the takeaway faster to understand because the viewer can immediately see which ranks are below the baseline. At the same time, choosing Police Officer as the baseline is not neutral; it pushes the reader to treat that group as the standard that everyone else should be compared against.
  • Score: -1.5 - I combined the highest titles into a Captain+ group and arranged the chart like a rank ladder. This gives the visualization a cleaner story about power and protection, but it also hides some messier details inside the top-rank group. In particular, it keeps the Deputy Inspector pattern from standing out as much as it would on its own.
  • Score: -1.0 - I used red tones and a pretty direct title because I wanted the chart to feel urgent. Those choices nudge the viewer toward seeing the pattern as a warning sign before they look closely at every number. I still avoided words like "proof" or "cover-up" because the data does not go that far.
  • Score: -1.5 - I left sample sizes out of the main chart. That makes the Captain+ estimate feel more comparable to the Police Officer estimate than it really is, since the groups are very different in size. I made that choice because it helps the persuasive argument, but I still included a source note so the transformation was not completely hidden.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Score: +2.0 - I showed every rank with at least 50 allegation records and labeled the sample sizes. This is less dramatic than the FOR chart, but it gives the viewer important context. It also makes it harder to treat a small group and a large group as if they carry the same amount of evidence.
  • Score: +2.0 - I added 95% confidence intervals to the substantiation rates. The intervals show that the higher-rank estimates are less stable because there are fewer cases. This does not make the rank differences disappear, but it does make the viewer more cautious about reading too much into each point.
  • Score: +1.5 - I used a zero-based y-axis and added an overall reference line. The zero baseline keeps the differences from looking larger than they are, while the reference line gives the viewer a straightforward comparison point. I thought about using a tighter scale, but that would have made this side feel less honest.
  • Score: +1.0 - I titled the chart "the clean rank protection ladder breaks" instead of saying the system is fair. That wording still makes an argument, but it does not pretend the data proves the complaint process works perfectly. It only argues that the simple rank-protection claim is not as solid as it first appears.

Final Reflection

The easiest part of the project was doing the basic calculations. The dataset already has each officer's rank at the time of the incident and the board disposition, so calculating substantiation rates by rank was not too difficult. The harder part was deciding what those rates actually meant. Each row is an allegation record, not a perfect record of every complaint experience, and one complaint can create more than one row. The dataset also comes from ProPublica's release of officers who were still on the force as of June 2020 and had at least one substantiated allegation. Because of those limits, it felt wrong to make a huge claim like "the system is corrupt," even though some of the rank comparisons can be made to look very convincing.

What surprised me most was how easy it was to make the same numbers tell two different stories. If I hid sample sizes, grouped the top ranks, and used stronger wording, the chart seemed to say that higher-ranking officers were being protected. If I showed the denominators, confidence intervals, and all qualifying ranks, the message changed to something more cautious: the evidence is not strong enough to prove a simple rank-protection pattern. I did not have to fake any numbers for either side. The story changed because of choices about the baseline, grouping, color, labels, and how much context I let the viewer see.

After this project, I think ethical visualization is not just about avoiding math mistakes or using the correct chart type. It is also about being honest about the choices behind the chart. A persuasive visual can be fair if it makes a clear argument while still giving readers enough context to question it. It becomes misleading when it leaves out information that would probably change how someone interprets the result, such as sample sizes, uncertainty, excluded categories, or dataset limitations. For that reason, I think the AGAINST visualization is more ethically defensible, even though the FOR visualization is more attention-grabbing at first.
